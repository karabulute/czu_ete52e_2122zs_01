According to the 1921 census, there were 19,480 inhabitants in 1,134 houses, of which 10,726 were women. 862 inhabitants declared their Czechoslovak nationality, 17 173 to German and 133 to Jewish. 16,201 Roman Catholics, 901 Evangelicals, 65 members of the Czechoslovak Hussite Church and 2,115 Jews lived here. According to the 1930 census, there were 23,901 inhabitants in 1,498 houses. 1,446 inhabitants declared their Czechoslovak nationality and 20,856 German. 19,718 Roman Catholics, 1,099 Evangelicals, 141 members of the Czechoslovak Hussite Church and 2,120 Jews lived here.

The German population, which made up the vast majority of the city before World War II , was displaced after the war and their property confiscated on the basis of Beneš's decrees.

Until the outbreak of World War II, the city had one of the most culturally and economically important Jewish communities in all of Czechoslovakia. For example, the world-famous Moser glass-works were founded by the Jewish businessman Ludwig Moser and run by his descendants until World War II. The local synagogue, burned down during the so-called Crystal Night , was one of the most magnificent buildings of its kind in Europe. The vast majority of Karlovy Vary Jews perished during the Holocaust.

As of January 2014, the population has dropped by less than 4,000 since the previous January, a drop below the 50,000 limit for the city means the loss of at least three representatives and also less money from the budget determination of taxes. Karlovy Vary survived with a population of over 50,000 only thanks to foreigners. The share of foreigners in the population of the Karlovy Vary region in 2012 was around seven percent, after Prague it is the highest share in the Czech Republic. The most numerous group of foreigners were Vietnamese, followed by Germans, Russians and Ukrainians.
